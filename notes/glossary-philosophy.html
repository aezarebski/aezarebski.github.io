  • Anselm's ontological argument
    • A flawed argument for the existence of god: a) god exists in the mind as the best thing that can be imagined, b) god would be better if he existed, therefore god exists.
  • Body theory (of identity)
    • It is existance in the same body that forms out identity, in contrast to the memory theory of identity.
  • Duty-based ethics (deontological ethics)
    • Ethics based on the assumption that some actions are intrinsically good/bad regardless of their outcome and that moral agents should only carry out good actions. Kant's ethics is viewed as an example of duty-based ethics.
    • In duty-based ethics, the rights of a moral agent are derived from the duties that everyone has, eg I have a right to live because moral agents have a duty not to kill me.
  • Epistemic responsibility
    • The responsibility for our own beliefs. Some have argued that we have a moral responsibility that we should only believe things that we have evidence for which clashes with the views of some religious people who view blind faith as a virtue.
  • Fideism
    • The position that some truths are best arrived at via faith rather than reason, used as an argument for belief in God. A useful quote from Kant is "deny knowledge in order to make room for faith".
  • Free will defence
    • An attempt to answer the problem of evil summarised by the following quote: "A world containing creatures who are significantly free (and freely perform more good than evil actions) is more valuable, all else being equal, than a world containing no free creatures at all. Now God can create free creatures, but He can't cause or determine them to do only what is right."1
  • George Berkeley (1685–1753)
    • Responded to John Locke by saying there are no primary qualities, only secondary ones and that everything only continues to exist because it is being perceived by God.
  • Gettier problem
    • Counter examples to the model of knowledge as a justified true belief. For example, Alice sees a clock that says it is two o'clock and as a result believes it is two o'clock, which it in fact is. She has a justified true belief that it is two o'clock. However, the clock actually stopped twelve hours ago. Gettier would have claimed that she does not have knowledge that it is two o'clock.
  • John Locke (1632–1704)
    • Suggested object have primary and secondary qualities, the former being intrinsic to the objects and the latter only existing due to the object being perceived by people. See George Berkeley for a response.
  • Memory theory (of identity)
    • Our identity is defined as the being the holder of our memories, in contrast to the body theory.
  • Teleological argument
    • Intelligent design argument for god.
  • Theodicy
    • An answer to the question of why a good God permits the manifestation of evil, thus resolving the issue of the problem of evil.
  • Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)
    • Provided five arguments for the existence of god. The first four form the cosmological arguments and basically involve invoking god as a way to avoid an infinite regress. The fifth argument is the teleological argument, later popularised by William Paley.
